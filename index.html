~ Mother's Day, the tenth of May, two thousand twenty-six ~

For Carrie

— Our Mama —
Dearest Carrie,

There are people who show up for the big moments. There are people who show up for the small ones. And then there is you — who somehow manages to show up for every single moment, even the ones nobody else notices, even the ones where showing up costs you more than you let on.

This letter is our attempt to name that. To say it out loud, in a way that doesn't disappear after breakfast.

~ From Mike ~

Carrie, I want to thank you for everything you do — for me, for Kelsey, for Kenzie, for Brandon. For the patience you have brought to the chaos of the last several years: making a life in the United States, supporting me through my career while you were quietly building a remarkable one of your own, and turning a house in a new country into a true home.

That last part is not a small thing. You made it a home. Not just a place where we live — a place where we belong.

I want to say this clearly, on the record: I appreciate your patience with me as I navigate new paths. I am chaotic. You know this better than anyone. You are honest with me when I need honesty, and underneath all of it is something that matters enormously to me — you trust me, and you always have. I do not take that lightly.

I want to talk about your work for a moment, because it deserves to be talked about.

Your career — the work you've built with the State at DCYF — asks something of you that most jobs simply do not. You show up every single day and you absorb the weight of other people's hardest moments. You do it professionally, carefully, and with the kind of genuine care that cannot be faked and cannot be trained into someone who doesn't already have it. You have it. Most people have no idea what that work actually asks of a person. I do. I see you come home from it. I see what it costs. And I see that you go back — that you keep going back — because somewhere underneath the difficulty, you find meaning in it. That is not a small thing either. That is a life built with purpose.

And then there was yesterday.

Visiting you at work for Schleby's going-away lunch — watching the people who surround you every day, the warmth in that room, the charcuterie board that was frankly an architectural achievement — I was reminded again of something I already knew but don't say enough: you earn the respect and love of the people around you. Genuinely. The gathering for Schleby was a reflection of the kind of colleague and friend and person you are. The kindness you put into your relationships comes back to you, and watching that room yesterday I was so proud of you. Proud of the career you have built, proud of the connections you have made, proud of the person you show up as every day.

Schleby — Shelby — came into your work world and became something closer to family. Almost like a daughter you gained through the particular alchemy of shared hard work and genuine care. The going-away for her was as much a celebration of you — of what you create in the spaces you inhabit — as it was a sendoff for her. Dallas got a good one. But so did you, for a good long while.

Losing her to Dallas stinks. Yes — stinks. With the K. You know exactly how I mean it.

And then there is Tara — Tatonka — your barre-class partner in crime, your East Coast kindred spirit, your friend in the truest sense of the word. The way you two show up for each other in the early-morning hours, week after week, is its own quiet kind of love story. The truth is the way you love your good friends — Schleby, Tatonka, the whole circle — is one of the most quietly remarkable things about you. You don't perform friendship. You practice it. The way you remember the small specific things about the people you care about is not by accident. That's who you are. And the people who get to be on the receiving end of it are unbelievably lucky.

I love you, Carrie. Deeply, fully, and with complete awareness of how lucky I am.

~ From Kelsey ~

Mom.

Here are a few things I like about you. And I really mean it.

You come to my soccer games. You show up for my school celebrations and everything that matters to me, which makes it matter more. You support my interests — all of them, even the ones that are probably a lot to keep up with.

I like how you make sure I have everything I need. Not just the practical stuff, but the other kind too. You tell me when I'm doing something wrong or should do it differently, and even when I don't act like I appreciate it, I do. You are making sure I'm not being a bad person. That's actually one of the most important things a mom can do, and you do it right.

I love our time together. The nails, the shopping, the coffee shops, trying new things. Those are some of my favourite things and they are our things. And Survivor — watching Survivor when Dad's away, watching it especially without Dad — that's ours too.

I also like bugging you about your ogre toes. I'm including that because it's true and it's funny and you know I love you.

You are my mom. Fully, specifically, mine. And I love you so much.

P.S.  I got you something for Mother's Day that I think we are both going to love — and that you might be slightly terrified of. Mother-daughter yoga. A new thing to try together. More laughs, more memories, more of our things. Just maybe try not to toot too much. I say this with love. 🌸

~ What You Have Built ~

You didn't build a house. You built a home.

The difference matters enormously and is almost impossible to explain to someone who hasn't lived in both. A house has walls and a roof. A home has a quality of air — something that tells you when you walk through the door that you are safe here, that you are known here, that you are allowed to be whoever you actually are. You made that. In Portland. Before that in Lethbridge. In every space you have brought yourself into and decided, quietly, that it was going to be a good place.

You are the reason that quality of air exists in this family. You are the reason Kelsey grew up feeling safe enough to be herself. You are the reason we all come home.

~ The Calls You Keep Making ~

There's something I want to say, because I don't think I've ever said it plainly enough.

The way you love your family back home is something I admire deeply. The distance between Vancouver and Nova Scotia is real — three thousand miles and three time zones of it — and you carry that distance with a kind of patience most people would have given up on a long time ago. You are relentless about staying connected to Grampy and your brother. You keep the relationships moving even when the calls don't always come back as often as they go out. You ask about Connor and Kaleigh. You stay interested in their lives — your nephew and your niece, growing up across a continent — and you make sure they know they have an aunt who hasn't forgotten them.

And with Grampy — through everything, through the new relationship and the shifting priorities and the moments that have stung in their own quiet way — you keep loving him. You keep loving your brother and his family. With everything you have, even when it isn't always easy.

That kind of unilateral love — the kind that doesn't keep score, the kind that just keeps showing up — is rare. I see it. I respect it. And I know it sometimes wears on you in ways you don't talk about, which is exactly why I'm putting it on the page.

I'm looking forward to Nova Scotia this summer. To booking the trip, to being back there with you, to seeing your family in their place. Whatever shape it takes — that time matters, and I can't wait to spend it with you.

~ A Word About Shelly ~

There is someone else who belongs in this letter. Who belongs in every letter you ever receive on Mother's Day.

Shelly. Grammy.

You and your mother traveled a complicated road, the way mothers and daughters sometimes do. But you found each other — as adults, as two women who chose to meet each other differently — and what you built in those years was real and it was earned. Those phone calls on the drive into work in the morning, Lethbridge, her voice on the line, just the two of you — that was a love language you created together. Not every daughter and mother get there. You did.

Shelly was Grammy to Brandon, to Mackenzie, to Kelsey. All three of them, fully, without qualification. She showed up for them with the proud love that grandmothers carry, and she chose that love — it wasn't assigned to her by biology, it was something she decided. That says everything.

She was proud of you. Enormously, specifically proud of you — even when it came out sideways, even when the words didn't quite match the feeling underneath. She saw the career. She saw the family. She saw the woman you were becoming. She knew.

She would be impossibly proud of you today.

We are saying it on her behalf and ours.

~ Solo, Stormy & Chewy say Happy Mother's Day too ~

Solo — your boy, your shadow, your small dignified presence who chose you completely and has never once reconsidered — would like you to know that the spot beside you on the couch is his favourite place in the entire world. He does not say this out loud. He says it by being there.

Stormy would like you to know that she loves you, and also that she would very much like a snack, and that these two things are equally important to her and she sees no contradiction in that.

And then there is Chewy. Where do I even start.

Chewy is here because of you. Period.

We had just lost Schooner — the best boy a dog has ever been, the one who had been at my side from before you and I even met, who got old the way the great ones do, slowly and then all at once. He was gone. We were grieving. And we still had Orbit at home — our big fluffy floof of a dog who loves food more than anything or anybody on this earth. So in my official position at the time, the idea of bringing in another dog — let alone a tiny new puppy — was going to happen over my dead body.

You knew better. You went and got that little puppy schnauzer anyway. You knew exactly what this family needed before I did. You were right, and I was wrong, and I will admit that here on parchment, in cursive, to the universe and everyone in it.

Chewy is the best little puppy dog miniature schnauzer in the entire world. She watches sci-fi with me. She runs midnight security patrols. She has waged a years-long, unsuccessful, completely committed war against a backyard squirrel. She is one of my best friends in this whole life — and she would not be in it if you hadn't insisted, gently and unmovably, that she belonged here.

Bringing her home is one of the greatest things you have ever done. I mean that. Thank you for not listening to me.

Chewy loves you with the full and uncomplicated loyalty that only a dog can offer. She thinks you are the best one — but she would like that kept between you.

~ The Things We Don't Say Enough ~

You show up. Not just when it's convenient. When it costs something.

You are funnier than people realize. The people who actually know you know.

You carry more than you let on. We see it, even when we don't always say so.

You love deeply and specifically. It shows in the rituals — the mornings, the pedicures, the garden, the early bedtimes that restore you so you can keep giving. Love is a practice for you, not just a feeling.

Your work matters. More than most people's work matters. You chose one of the hardest things a person can do and you do it with care, every single day.

You made this family possible. That is not an exaggeration. That is just true.

Shelly would be proud. She already was. We're saying it louder now.

We hope you love a few of your Mother's Day gifts today — and that the yoga brings you and Kelsey something new to laugh about together. One more layer on top of the coffee shops and the manicures and the shopping and all the things that are yours.

You deserve every single one of them.

Happy Mother's Day, Carrie.

You are so deeply, completely loved.

With everything we've got —
Mike & Kelsey
